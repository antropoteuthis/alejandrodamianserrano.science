Conference Talks & Presentations

UO Biology Symposium, 2022, Eugene, OR

The development, evolution, and locomotory implications of salp colony architecture

Salps are urochordates that filter-feed on microbial production in marine pelagic ecosystems. They form colonies of asexually-budded individuals which swim by multi-jet propulsion. Colonies develop into species-specific architectures with distinct zooid orientations including transversal, oblique, linear, helical, bipinnate chains, whorls, and clusters. These architectures vary in frontal drag, thrust ratio, and locomotory efficiency.

We (1) defined the salp colony morphospace, (2) characterized developmental pathways, (3) assessed hydrodynamic consequences for locomotion, and (4) reconstructed evolutionary history. Linear chains are fastest and most efficient; clusters are slowest. Results suggest locomotory efficiency may be driven by ecological trade-offs rather than strong selection.

Yale Peabody Museum, 2021, New Haven, CT

Shaped to Kill: The Evolution of Siphonophores’ Secret Weapons for Prey Capture in the Open Ocean

Predatory specialization often evolves through modifications in the prey-capture apparatus. In siphonophores, modular structures used exclusively for prey capture allow evolution of generalism and new prey-type specializations. My research demonstrated convergent modifications of morphology, shifts toward different optima, and evolutionary correlations between parts of the stinging batteries, revealing novel patterns and mechanisms in specialization that shape oceanic food-web structure.

Evolution 2019, Providence, RI

The evolution of siphonophore tentacles as tools for prey capture

Siphonophores possess complex nematocyst batteries on side branches called tentilla, the principal organs for prey capture. This study describes tentilla morphology and nematocysts, identifies evolutionary patterns, and links them to predatory specialization. Isolation of the prey-capture apparatus allows extreme morphological adaptations for dietary specialization, critical to understanding the role of morphological diversity in food webs.

eDSBS, 2020

Characterizing the Diets of Siphonophores using DNA Metabarcoding

Siphonophores are abundant predators in the Offshore Central California Current ecosystem. We performed DNA metabarcoding of 27 species’ gut contents, collected via blue water dives and ROVs. Analysis of six barcode markers along the 18S gene linked prey OTUs to a local zooplankton database. Results reveal hidden midwater trophic links and highlight the importance of local species diversity for nutrient flow and ecosystem function.
